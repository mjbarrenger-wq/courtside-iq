# Courtside IQ – Writing Standards Reference
*Last updated: 2026-06-05 | Applies to: coaching insights, driver summaries, player reports, dashboard copy, product and commercial communications*

---

## Purpose

This file covers two distinct writing contexts for Courtside IQ, each with its own tone and intent:

1. **Coaching Insights & In-App Writing** — AI-generated performance analysis, driver summaries, player reports, recommendations. Tone: clear, direct, practically grounded, like a knowledgeable analyst talking to a coach.
2. **Product & Commercial Writing** — external communications, pitch materials, partnership outreach. Tone: professionally authoritative, commercially credible, measured.

Both contexts share the same human-first principles. Neither should read as AI-generated or data-system output.

---

# Part 1 — Coaching Insights & In-App Writing

*Applies to: AI coaching insights, driver summaries, player reports, development priorities, dashboard copy, training recommendations*

All coaching-facing content produced by Courtside IQ must sound like it comes from a knowledgeable basketball analyst or performance coach — someone who has watched the film, understands the game, and knows what matters at training. Practical, direct, and grounded in what actually happened.

The primary test: does this help a coach decide what to work on?

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Lead With the Action

Coaching insights tell a coach what to do. The instruction comes first. The data observation follows, briefly, if it adds something the instruction alone does not.

**Avoid:** "Ball security is the clearest leak in the offence. Turnovers are costing possessions. Coaches should consider prioritising live-ball decision-making in practice."

**Write:** "Run live-ball decision drills at game speed with active defenders on every rep. Two-on-one, tight spaces, no uncontested dribbles. That is where turnover habits get fixed."

---

### 2. Use Imperative Sentences

Coaches instruct. They do not describe what should happen — they direct it.

**Avoid:** "Players should focus on boxing out before looking for the rebound."

**Write:** "Box out before you look for the ball. Two hands on your player first. Make it a non-negotiable in every defensive possession drill."

Short imperatives carry authority. Long explanations undermine it. Coaching cues rarely need more than eight words.

---

### 3. Be Drill and Constraint Specific

Vague training recommendations are not actionable. Name the situation, the constraint, or the condition that forces the behaviour you want. Constraints teach faster than instruction alone.

**Avoid:** "Work on defensive positioning at training."

**Write:** "Set up a 3v3 shell drill at the start of every session. Ball goes to the corner — everyone rotates, closes out, hands active. Run it until the reads are automatic. Then go 5v5."

---

### 4. Two Registers — Know Which One You Are In

**Team insights (coach-facing):** Address the head coach as a peer. Speak from performance data directly to the person making decisions. "Your turnover rate over the last five games is 26%. Run live-ball pressure drills this week."

**Player development notes (player-facing):** Address the player by first name. Direct, specific, confident. "Cooper, your offensive rebound rate is the highest on the squad. Push it further by positioning before the shot goes up, not after."

Never mix registers within the same content block.

---

### 5. Use Basketball Vocabulary

Use the sport's language. Not jargon — the real basketball terms coaches use daily on the court.

Preferred vocabulary: half-court, transition, shell drill, gap coverage, closeout, contest, kick-out, catch-and-shoot, off-the-dribble, ball reversal, paint touch, post-entry, live-ball, help side, weak side, press break, ball handler, wing, corner, elbow, high-low, drive-and-kick, paint collapse.

When basketball language exists, use it. "Defensive positioning" → "gap coverage" or "help-side position". "Pressure" → "closeout" or "contest" depending on context.

---

### 6. Confident but Honest

Take a position. AI hedges everything with "may," "could," "appears to suggest." When the numbers point somewhere, say so directly.

**Use:** "The turnover rate is too high to sustain this win margin."

**Avoid:** overpromising, certainty beyond what the data supports, or absolute claims about future performance.

---

### 7. Connect Every Metric to Behaviour

Statistics are not the output. The behaviour behind the statistic is the output. Every metric must connect to a specific basketball behaviour or coaching concept.

**Avoid:** "TO% is 24.5%."

**Write:** "One possession in four ends in a turnover. Live-ball decision-making is the first place to look."

This is not just a style preference — it is the core purpose of the platform.

---

### 8. Age and Development Aware

Current pilot is U12. All recommendations must be developmentally appropriate, practically coachable, and realistic for 11 and 12 year olds.

**Avoid:** recommendations that presuppose elite-level execution, specialist skills, or unrealistic training volume.

---

## Formatting & Readability

### 9. Readability Rules

- Short paragraphs — 1–2 sentences default, 3 max
- Use bullet points only when listing discrete items — not as a default for any analysis
- Avoid dense text blocks
- Numbers as digits (3 possessions, 7 games, 24.5%)
- Headers in sentence case — not Title Case
- Bold sparingly: 1–2 key findings per section, not decoration

---

### 10. Specificity Over Generality

Generic observations are filler. Every insight should give a coach something specific: a drill, a constraint, a number, a player name.

**Avoid:** "The team has shown good defensive intensity."

**Write:** "The defensive rebound rate is 79% across 29 games. That consistency is the floor — protect it by running your box-out rule at every training, not just the ones where you remember."

---

## Final Test (Coaching Content)

Before any coaching insight or driver summary is presented, ask:

> *Could a coach read this and immediately know what to run at Tuesday's training?*

If it reads like a statistics report, rewrite it. If it gives an observation without an action, add the action. If it hedges where the data is clear, cut the hedge.

# Part 2 — Product & Commercial Writing

*Applies to: pitch materials, investor communications, partnership outreach, website copy, product descriptions*

---

## Purpose

External-facing writing about Courtside IQ should make coaches, clubs, and potential partners feel they are hearing from people who genuinely understand basketball and coaching — not a technology company pitching a product.

The goal: someone reads it and thinks *"these people understand what coaches actually need."*

---

## Core Tone: Professional · Knowledgeable · Approachable · Direct

**Professional** — accurate, consistent, commercially credible. Still a product.

**Knowledgeable** — write with genuine authority on basketball performance, coaching workflows, and what analytics does and does not tell you.

**Approachable** — no intimidating jargon, no overclaiming. Easy to engage with for a grassroots coach who has never used an analytics platform.

**Direct** — state the value plainly. Coaches are busy. Say what the platform does and why it matters without burying it in product language.

---

## Tone in Practice

### 9. Write to One Person

Even in broad-reach content, write as if talking to one specific coach — a community-level basketball coach trying to figure out what to work on before Tuesday's training. This creates warmth and directness.

**Instead of:** *"Courtside IQ provides a comprehensive performance intelligence framework for basketball organisations."*

**Write:** *"Most coaches already know something is off. Courtside IQ tells you exactly what — and where to start."*

---

### 10. Lead With the Coaching Problem

Every coach has a practical problem: understanding why they're winning or losing and knowing what to fix. Start with that, not with the platform.

**Instead of:** *"Courtside IQ is an AI-powered basketball analytics platform."*

**Write:** *"A 22-win season still leaves questions. Why does the turnover rate spike in close games? Which defensive habit is actually winning possessions? Courtside IQ works backwards from the outcome to find the answer."*

---

### 11. Show Expertise Without Lecturing

Don't explain what analytics is. Show what it does in specific, practical terms — so it reads as genuine knowledge rather than a product brochure.

**Good:** *"The driver tree breaks the game into eight performance categories. Each one connects directly to Net PPP, so coaches can see not just what happened, but how much it mattered."*

**Avoid:** *"Our proprietary analytics engine leverages advanced data modelling to deliver actionable basketball intelligence."*

---

### 12. Keep It Conversational — Not Casual

Contractions are fine. Direct address is fine. Short sentences are fine. Slang, informality, or over-familiarity are not.

Aim for the tone of a confident performance analyst who can also talk to a parent on the sideline.

---

### 13. Basketball Is the Common Ground

Courtside IQ exists because basketball coaching is hard and the tools available to grassroots coaches are inadequate. When that's natural to acknowledge, do it — as shared experience, not as a marketing angle.

*"Stats apps tell you what happened. Most coaches already know something went wrong — they just need to know where to look first."* — Credible.
*"We're passionate about basketball!"* — Hollow.

---

### 14. Friendly Doesn't Mean Gushing

No excessive enthusiasm. No hollow affirmations. No promises that can't be kept.

**Avoid:**
- "Absolutely!"
- "Groundbreaking analytics!"
- "Transform your coaching journey!"

**Use instead:**
- Calm, specific language that just gets on with it
- Warmth through word choice and rhythm — not enthusiasm markers

---

## Final Test (Commercial Content)

Before publishing or sending, ask:

> *Would a basketball coach read this and feel like they've heard from people who understand what they're dealing with — or does it sound like a tech startup trying to sell them something?*

If it sounds like a startup pitch, rewrite it.

# Part 3 — Anti-AI Writing Rules (Universal)

*These rules apply across all Courtside IQ writing — coaching insights and commercial content. They exist because AI-generated text has identifiable patterns. Any of these in a draft signals a rewrite is needed.*

---

## Punctuation

### 15. No Dashes as Punctuation

**Do not use hyphens ("-") or em dashes ("—") as punctuation marks within sentences.**

Dashes used as sentence breaks fragment the rhythm and make writing feel clipped or AI-generated.

**Instead:** restructure the sentence, use a comma, a colon, parentheses, or break into two sentences.

**Avoid:** *"The defensive rebound rate is strong — it's the backbone of their game."*
**Write:** *"The defensive rebound rate is the backbone of their game."*

**Note:** Hyphens in compound words remain correct and required (e.g. "play-by-play", "mid-range", "well-coached"). This rule applies only to dashes used as punctuation marks.

---

## The Banned List

### 16. Dead AI Vocabulary

These words are statistically overrepresented in AI output. They are the fingerprint of machine-generated text. Never use them.

delve, realm, harness, unlock, tapestry, paradigm, cutting-edge, revolutionize, landscape (abstract), intricate/intricacies, showcasing, crucial, pivotal, surpass, meticulously, vibrant, unparalleled, underscore (verb), leverage, synergy, innovative, game-changer, testament, commendable, meticulous, highlight (verb), emphasize, boast, groundbreaking, align, foster, showcase, enhance, holistic, garner, accentuate, pioneering, trailblazing, unleash, versatile, transformative, redefine, seamless, optimize, scalable, robust, breakthrough, empower, streamline, frictionless, elevate, adaptive, effortless, data-driven, insightful, proactive, mission-critical, visionary, disruptive, reimagine, unprecedented, intuitive, leading-edge, synergize, democratize, accelerate, state-of-the-art, dynamic, immersive, predictive, transparent, proprietary, integrated, plug-and-play, turnkey, future-proof, paradigm-shifting, supercharge, enduring, interplay, valuable, captivate

Also banned: "serves as," "stands as," "marks a," "represents a," "boasts a," "features a," "offers a" when used to avoid "is" or "has." Just say "is."

---

### 17. Dead Phrases

- "In today's [anything]..."
- "It's important to note that..." / "It's worth noting..."
- "In order to" (just say "to")
- "I'd be happy to help"
- "Straightforward"
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's explore" / "Let's unpack" / "Delve into"
- "At the end of the day"
- "Moving forward"
- "To put this in perspective..."
- "What makes this particularly interesting is..."
- "The implications here are..."
- "In other words..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "Here's the part nobody's talking about" / "What nobody tells you"
- "In this section, I will..." (all meta commentary about what you're about to say)
- "Despite its [positive words], [subject] faces challenges..."
- "Challenges and Future Prospects" as a section header

---

### 18. Dead Transitions

- "Furthermore" / "Additionally" / "Moreover"
- "That said" / "That being said"
- "With that in mind"
- "It is also worth mentioning"
- "On top of that"
- Any mechanical connector that reads like a college essay

---

### 19. Engagement Bait

- "Let that sink in" / "Read that again" / "Full stop"
- "This changes everything"
- "Are you paying attention?" / "You're not ready for this"

---

### 20. Hype Language

- "Supercharge" / "Unlock" / "Future-proof"
- "10x your [anything]"
- "Game-changer" / "Cutting-edge"
- Any promise of easy outcomes or transformation

This applies with particular force to basketball analytics. The platform helps coaches make better decisions. It does not "revolutionise" anything.

---

### 21. Negative Parallelisms — FATAL (Rewrite the Entire Sentence)

This is the single most reliable tell of AI-generated text. AI is addicted to these because they make shallow ideas sound profound.

**Banned patterns:**
- "This isn't X. This is Y."
- "Not X. Y."
- "Forget X. This is Y."
- "Less X, more Y."
- "Not only X, but also Y."
- "It's not just about X, it's about Y."
- "No X, no Y, just Z."
- "Stop thinking X. Start thinking Y."
- "X is dead. Y is the future."
- "The question isn't X. The question is Y."
- "You don't need X. You need Y."
- ANY sentence that negates one framing then asserts a corrected one.

**Watch for the disguised versions:**
- "While X might seem right, Y is actually..." (same pattern)
- "Sure, X works. But Y is where the real..." (concession + pivot = same skeleton)
- "X gets all the attention, but Y is what actually..." (same thing, third disguise)

**The fix:** delete everything before the positive claim. If you wrote "It's not about the stats. It's about the behaviour" — just write "It's about the behaviour." The negated framing adds nothing.

---

## AI Writing Patterns to Avoid

### 22. Significance Inflation

AI inflates the importance of everything: "a pivotal moment in the evolution of..." / "marking a significant shift toward..." / "setting the stage for..."

State the fact. Let the reader judge significance.

---

### 23. Rule of Three

AI defaults to listing exactly 3 things: "speed, efficiency, and innovation." It uses this to make shallow analysis look comprehensive.

Use 2 things. Or 4. Or just say the one thing that matters.

---

### 24. False Ranges

"From individual skills to team systems." Sounds comprehensive, means nothing. If you can't identify meaningful ground between X and Y, the range is fake. Delete it. Be specific about one thing.

---

### 25. Elegant Variation (Forced Synonyms)

AI's repetition penalty forces it to swap terms: a player becomes "the athlete," then "the key contributor," then "the individual in question."

Use the name again. Forced synonyms are worse than repetition.

---

### 26. Meta Commentary

"In this section, we will discuss..." / "Let me walk you through..." / "Here's a comprehensive overview of..."

Say the thing. Don't announce that you're about to say it.

---

### 27. Superficial Analysis via Participle Phrases

AI attaches -ing phrases to create fake depth: "highlighting its importance," "underscoring its significance," "reflecting broader trends in the game."

Delete the participle phrase. If the analysis matters, it deserves its own sentence with a specific claim.

---

### 28. Collaborative Communication Leakage

"I hope this helps!" / "Certainly!" / "Of course!" / "Great question!" / "Would you like me to..."

Strip these from all application outputs.

---

### 29. Metronome Rhythm

Every sentence the same length. Every paragraph the same number of sentences. AI text has no texture.

Real writing breathes unevenly. Short. Then longer. Then a fragment. Then a sentence that earns its length.

---

## Applying These Rules

| Rule type | Application |
|---|---|
| **Hard rules** — banned words, banned patterns, no dashes | Never violate. Absolute. |
| **Strong tendency (70–80%)** — short paragraphs, direct address, active voice, specific data points, varied rhythm | Apply consistently. Override only with clear reason. |
| **Light preference** — specific word choices, structural choices | Use judgment. Let content dictate. |

The litmus test: *Does this sound like something a knowledgeable basketball analyst would say to a coach — or does it sound like an AI generating a performance report?*

If it feels generated, rewrite it.
